# Koe Sessions — Product Personas
*Version 1.0 · 2026-04-12*
*Five archetypes that define what Sessions must do — and why*

---

## Why these five

Sessions is not a recording app. It is a promise: the take that happened before anyone knew it was a take will never be lost again. These five personas represent every dimension of that promise — the bedroom songwriter, the touring band, the demo obsessive, the detail maximalist, and the venue that turns spontaneity into a product.

Design decisions that don't serve at least one of these five should not be made.

---

## Persona 01 — Ed Sheeran
### Solo singer-songwriter, loop-builder, home recorder

**Context:** Ed Sheeran built "Divide" from home recordings. His process is a loop pedal, an acoustic guitar, and a voice. He builds a loop, finds the groove three seconds in, and until Sessions, that three-second runway was always lost. He presses record after the moment has already happened.

**Work style:**
- Records alone, at any hour
- Loop structure: lay down guitar → voice on top → stack layers
- Home studio doubles as bedroom; the "studio" is wherever the guitar is
- Phones in demos to himself at 2am; many of those never get revisited
- Values the rawness of take-one, unintentional arrangements

**What he values most:**
- Spontaneity — the feeling that the song came through him, not from him
- Intimacy — just him, the guitar, the room
- Authenticity — he can tell when something was "produced" rather than discovered

**Pain:**
He has pressed record on a loop too late more times than he can count. The groove was already locked in at beat 3. By the time the DAW was open, or the voice memo was rolling, he had already played the best 5 seconds. He went back and tried to recreate it. It was never the same.

**Sessions solves this by:**
- The 5-second lookback means beat 1 is always already in the buffer
- No button required — the moment he picks up the guitar and starts a loop, Sessions is already behind him by five seconds
- He never has to "go into record mode" mentally. The session is already a session.

**Quote:**
> "The take that happened before I knew it was a take."

**Success condition:**
Ed plays something home-alone at 11pm that he's forgotten by morning. At 8am, Sessions Morning Mix surfaces it. He listens to 30 seconds and texts his producer immediately.

## Persona 02 — ONE OK ROCK
### Japanese rock band, global touring, 4 musicians

**Members:**
- Taka — vocals, writes melodies in his head between cities
- Toru — lead guitar, arrives at riffs through 45-minute exploration jams
- Ryota — bass, quiet listener who hears harmonic ideas others miss
- Tomoya — drums, thinks in time and synchronization

**Context:** They stay in Airbnb houses and hotels between shows. They are not in a studio. They have no gear beyond what they traveled with. But a guitar appears, someone starts playing, and within 20 minutes the room is a rehearsal. Nobody recorded it. They fly out the next morning.

**Work style:**
- Spontaneous hotel/house jams, usually after dinner, often after a few drinks
- No one sets up a mobile studio — it's acoustic guitar and voices
- Ideas surface naturally in group conversation; no formal "writing session"
- Their best material often emerges from the loosest settings
- Label wants stems; management wants a clear multi-track of anything significant

**What they need:**
- Multi-track stems for producer and label (not a mono voice memo)
- Automatic sync across every device in the house — if Tomoya is in one room and Toru is in another, both rooms should be on the same timeline
- No setup, ever — nobody is going to configure a recording rig after a flight

**Pain:**
Every room they've stayed in has had music in the walls that never got captured. An idea Taka hummed in a Nagoya hotel in 2019 became the bridge of a song two years later — but the original was never recorded, and what ended up on the album is a reconstruction from memory, not the real thing.

**Sessions solves this by:**
- Amps in every room form a synchronized multi-track the moment one instrument sounds
- The stems are production-ready: 48kHz / 24-bit, labeled by room, synced to <2ms
- No one has to designate a "producer" for the evening — Sessions is ambient infrastructure, not a tool

**Quote:**
> "Every room we've stayed in has had music left in the walls."

**Success condition:**
After a stay, the tour manager receives a Sessions export with 6 labeled stems, timestamped from 11:47pm in a Kyoto house. Two months later, one of those stems becomes the intro of a single.

## Persona 03 — Taylor Swift
### Pop-country songwriter, prolific demo writer, home studio owner

**Context:** Taylor Swift writes 10 songs to finish one. She has a home studio called "The Bunker." She writes constantly, at all hours, in all modes — at the piano, on the couch with a guitar, in the car humming to voice memos. She produces demos at a rate that outpaces any formal session.

**Work style:**
- Writes alone first; collaborators come after the skeleton exists
- Morning is her most generative time — she often has a melody idea before coffee
- Drafts pile up; most are abandoned not because they're bad but because she forgot them
- A large % of her "best work" is ideas she circled back to 6 months later
- Her production instinct is strong; she hears the finished arrangement in her head during the rough demo

**What she values most:**
- Volume — she needs to capture everything, because she doesn't know which 10% will matter
- Searchability — she wants to find that thing she hummed on a Wednesday in October
- Low friction — she has the studio, but she doesn't want to "go into session mode" for a couch idea
- Draft integrity — the original feel of the demo matters; over-produced demos lose the core

**Pain:**
She writes songs the way other people have conversations. That means most ideas happen outside of "record mode" — at home, spontaneous, off the top. The ones she captures are lucky. The ones she doesn't are gone. Morning Mix could function as a creative audit: everything she made in the last 24 hours, surfaced as a playlist, waiting for her with coffee.

**Sessions solves this by:**
- Every room in The Bunker has continuous passive capture — the guitar on the couch, the piano in the corner, the vocal warm-up in the hallway
- Morning Mix is a curated daily highlight reel — Sessions' ML surfaces the most musically coherent moments from the night or morning session
- She never has to make a decision about whether something is "worth recording" — Sessions makes that decision unnecessary

**Quote:**
> "I write songs the way other people have conversations."

**Success condition:**
Taylor opens Morning Mix on a Tuesday and hears a guitar loop from 6am she doesn't consciously remember playing. She slows it down, hums a melody over it, and sends a voice note to her co-writer with the Sessions clip attached.

## Persona 04 — Jacob Collier
### Harmonic genius, multi-instrumentalist, bedroom studio architect

**Context:** Jacob Collier built his entire career in his bedroom, recording himself across 50+ tracks. He plays every instrument, layers harmonics that shouldn't work and do, and treats the room itself as part of the arrangement. He has said the room is an instrument. He means it literally.

**Work style:**
- Every track contains micro-detail decisions: pick attack on guitar, the resonance of the floor
- Uses the ambient sound of the room — the hum of an amp, the reverb of a stairwell — as texture
- Builds arrangements in layers; each layer is captured separately and brought into sync later
- Values the specificity of a single microphone position — a few centimeters changes everything
- His process is slow, deliberate, and deeply spatial

**What he values most:**
- Micro-detail capture — he wants the pick on the string AND the room resonance around it, as separate tracks
- Spatial accuracy — each Amp in the house captures a different distance and angle; together they form a 3D acoustic picture of the space
- Stems for layering — he doesn't mix in-session; he exports stems and sculpts them in post
- Room as instrument — Sessions captures the ambient acoustic of the room at all times, not just direct signal

**Pain:**
He has played something on guitar, then realized the room resonance behind him was the exact reverb the track needed — but he only had the direct piezo capture and the room recording started five seconds too late. The acoustic context was already in the air; the mic just wasn't rolling.

**Sessions solves this by:**
- The 5-second lookback means the room was already in the recording when the idea started
- Multiple Amps in the room each capture a unique acoustic position — near-field, mid-field, ambient — all synchronized
- The Pick captures contact vibration directly from the instrument body (pre-air, pre-room)
- Together: direct signal + instrument body resonance + near-field room + ambient room = a 4-layer spatial stem pack

**Quote:**
> "The room is an instrument too."

**Success condition:**
Jacob exports a Sessions stem pack from a home session and uses the mid-field Amp recording — not the direct pickup, not the DAW synth reverb — as the actual room reverb on the final mix of a track. He mentions this in an interview.

## Persona 05 — Boutique Hotel Host
### Aman Resorts, music-themed residency suite

**Context:** Aman Resorts and similar ultra-luxury properties are adding artist residency programs. A music-themed suite is worth $2,000–$8,000/night if it offers genuine creative value — not just a Yamaha piano and a Spotify playlist, but something no other hotel has. Koe Sessions becomes the suite's value proposition: "Your session is waiting for you at checkout."

**Business model:**
- Hotel licenses Koe Sessions for a specific suite or villa
- Recurring license fee per room per month (B2B SaaS, not consumer hardware sale)
- Guests sign a consent form at check-in (pre-loaded on the in-room iPad)
- At checkout, the front desk provides a private Sessions URL — all captures from the stay
- Hotel retains no rights; everything belongs to the artist

**What the hotel needs:**
- Zero-friction for staff — sessions run without anyone managing them
- Clear consent infrastructure — legal team needs documentation
- Brand differentiation — the "Sessions suite" is a marketing asset, not a spec sheet
- Reliability at scale — the suite must work for every guest, not just musicians who know how to configure things
- Business terms that are clean — licensing, not hardware sale; hotel can cancel

**What guests experience:**
- Check in. The suite already has Amps in every room.
- Play something. Anything. Even if it's just warming up before a show.
- Check out. Receive a private URL with everything.
- Six months later, the artist mentions in an interview: "I recorded part of this at a hotel in Bali."

**Pain:**
Aman offers $10,000/night villas. Their guests include touring artists who arrive between shows with instruments and ideas and nowhere to put them. The hotel cannot serve this creative need with a recording studio (too intrusive, too formal). They need ambient, invisible infrastructure.

**Sessions solves this by:**
- Ambient capture — the suite "just records" without staff involvement
- The business model works at scale: one license per suite, handled by an Account Manager, renewable annually
- Consent is systematized — the consent form is built into the check-in flow, not a separate friction point
- The URL at checkout is the deliverable — the hotel's "gift" to the artist

**Quote:**
> "Your session is waiting for you at checkout."

**Success condition:**
Aman Resorts licenses Sessions for one villa in Bali, one in Tokyo. Three artists in the first six months mention the hotel by name when discussing where they recorded material. The hotel renews. They ask about adding it to four more properties.

## What these personas collectively require

1. **The 5-second lookback must always work.** This is the non-negotiable core across 4 of 5 personas. If it fails once, trust breaks.

2. **Zero configuration.** Not "easy setup." Zero. None of these five will tolerate an onboarding flow.

3. **Production-quality output.** Every persona who receives stems will put them in a DAW. The recording must hold up at 48kHz/24-bit.

4. **Privacy that is structurally provable.** Taylor's catalog is worth billions. Aman's guests include active-contract artists. The privacy architecture must be verifiable, not promised.

5. **A single moment of delight, not a feature set.** The hotel guest checks out and gets a URL. Ed wakes up and hears the loop. Jacob opens the stems and finds the room reverb he needed. None of them need a feature list. They need one perfect moment.
